Oils

What are they?

Every culture and every household is familiar with oils. Canola oil, sunflower oil, olive oil, peanut oil, the list goes on and on. Oils are classified as any kind of fat that is in liquid form at room temperature. Some of these oils are healthy for us. They provide us with necessary nutrients and healthy fats. Some of them, however, when overly processed are dangerous. But despite this danger, they have become so widely used over the past few decades that they are largely inescapable. This class of oil is seed oils. Oils produced through the processing of sunflower seeds, rapeseeds, soybeans, corn, and more. Everyone can find these oils at their local market. For a few dollars you can bring home an entire gallon. But is there a hidden cost?

How are they made?

The production of these processed oils begins with the harvesting of the seed. The seeds will be shelled if they have a shell, and then conditioned through heating to prepare them for the first step of extraction. Pressing. The pressing extracts some of the oils out of the seeds. The dry mash is then broken down further and moved to the second kind of extraction. Hexane extraction. Hexane is mixed with the mash to produce a slurry. This slurry is then heated. Most of the hexane will be evaporated during this step. The oil (and small hexane leftovers) is then distilled, the hexane will be collected for repurposing, and the mash is sold for animal feed. Finally, all the oil is bleached using a bleaching clay, and then deodorized to remove and free fatty acids and volatile components that resulted from the extraction process. This leaves you with the colorless oil sold on the shelf.
